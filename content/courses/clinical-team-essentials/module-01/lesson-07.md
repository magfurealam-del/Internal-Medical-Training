# Module 1 — From Intact Skin to Wound Depth

**Vault source:** Course 1, Theoretical Module 1, Slides 1–8; Course 1 wound-assessment materials  
**Primary mechanism:** wound depth is a map of lost structure and lost function.

## A layered assessment

When assessing a wound, move through four questions:

1. **Surface:** Is the epidermal barrier disrupted? What is the moisture, color, and integrity state?
2. **Support:** Is the dermis involved? Is there loss of collagen structure, appendages, sensation, or vascular support?
3. **Cushion:** Is subcutaneous tissue exposed, lost, edematous, or undermined?
4. **Deep structures:** Are fascia, tendon, muscle, joint, or bone exposed or at risk?

This sequence supports accurate description, but it does not replace formal classification systems or specialist assessment.

## Why depth changes the plan

Depth changes the wound’s oxygen needs, bacterial exposure, mechanical stability, ability to contract, available epithelial sources, pain/sensation pattern, and risk of deep infection. A superficial defect and a deep wound cannot be treated as the same biological problem simply because they have similar surface dimensions.

## Documentation language

Prefer:

`Left plantar wound, 2.0 × 1.5 cm, visible tissue type documented, depth/undermining assessed or unable to assess, periwound condition described, sensation/perfusion/loading context recorded.`

Avoid:

`Large bad ulcer.`

### Doctor checkpoint

Integrate depth with etiology, perfusion, sensation, pressure, infection risk, and systemic context.

### Nurse checkpoint

Measure and describe consistently, identify change from baseline, and escalate uncertainty about depth or exposed structures.

## Professor's deep dive: depth is an anatomical hypothesis

Depth is not reliably inferred from color, pain or diameter. A small lesion may have a sinus, tunnel or puncture tract; a broad erosion may remain superficial. Establish the deepest visible or appropriately assessed structure and record what cannot be visualized. Epidermal loss can resurface; dermal loss requires matrix repair and often scar; subcutaneous loss changes force transmission; tendon, bone, joint or neurovascular exposure changes urgency and referral.

Document location, orientation, length, width, depth, tissue, edge, periwound, undermining, drainage, pain, sensation, perfusion, loading and trajectory. Use “not visualized” or “uncertain” honestly rather than inventing a stage or diagnosis.

Further reading: [NCBI wound assessment](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK482198/) and [NCBI wound-healing tissue response](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK3938/).

## Faculty teaching map

- **Describe the wound in layers:**
  - Surface: intact, eroded, blistered, macerated, or denuded.
  - Wound bed: epithelial tissue, granulation, slough, eschar, fat, tendon, muscle, or bone if visible.
  - Edge and periwound: rolled, undermined, macerated, callused, inflamed, fragile, or intact.
- **Separate observation from interpretation:**
  - “Yellow tissue visible” is an observation.
  - “Slough” is an interpretation that should be used when the appearance supports it.
  - “Infected” requires more than color alone.
- **State uncertainty safely:**
  - Use “not visualized” when tissue is covered.
  - Use “unable to assess” when pain, bleeding, or safety limits examination.
  - Escalate suspected deep or neurovascular involvement.

## Take-home rule

Size is one dimension. Depth and the function of the structures involved often determine risk more than surface area alone.
