# Lesson 7: Recognizing Barriers Without Jumping Ahead

**Source:** Module 1, Pages 35–134.

Course 1 teaches barrier recognition and reasoning. Detailed cleansing, debridement, dressing selection, condition-specific escalation, advanced diagnostics, devices, and recurrence prevention belong in later courses.

## Barrier recognition boundaries

| Barrier | Recognize in Course 1 | Defer |
|---|---|---|
| Poor perfusion | Perfusion may limit healing; assessment is needed | Detailed vascular pathway |
| Local hypoxia | Oxygenation may be a barrier | HBOT/IVT/Flow protocols |
| Biofilm/slough | Recurrent tissue-bed concern needs reassessment | Cleansing/debridement technique |
| Infection/inflammatory burden | Concerning signs need structured reassessment | Full infection pathway |
| Pressure/shear | Mechanical force may maintain the wound | Detailed offloading prescription |
| Edema/venous/lymphatic failure | Fluid and venous factors may impair healing | Compression/Airos protocol |
| Neuropathy | Protective sensation changes risk | Device and pain adjunct indications |
| Moisture imbalance | Wound/periwound environment may be damaged | Full MASD/dressing technique |
| Necrosis/eschar | Non-viable tissue requires assessment | Debridement method |
| Edge failure | Epibole, callus, undermining, non-advancement may stall healing | Procedural correction |

## Case: plantar wound

A plantar wound has callus, slough, weak pulses, and sandal walking. Course 1 does not ask the learner to name a device. It asks the learner to document the findings, identify mechanical stress, possible perfusion limitation, and tissue-bed concern, complete appropriate assessment within scope, communicate concerns, and define monitoring.

## Key takeaway

Recognize the barrier, but do not silently convert recognition into an unapproved procedure or pathway decision.
