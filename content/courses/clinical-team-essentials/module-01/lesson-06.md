# Lesson 6: Evidence, Uncertainty, and Safe Reasoning

**Source:** Module 1, Pages 32–34.

Clinical reasoning should separate observation, interpretation, source-supported evidence, and assumption. When information is incomplete, transparent uncertainty plus a plan is safer than an unsupported confident label.

## Five-part note

- **Finding:** What was observed or measured?
- **Interpretation:** What might it indicate?
- **Evidence:** What supports the interpretation?
- **Uncertainty:** What is not established?
- **Action:** What will be assessed, communicated, or monitored next?

### Doctor checkpoint

Use available evidence to support the clinical interpretation and identify when more assessment or specialist input is needed.

### Nurse checkpoint

Report observations accurately and avoid converting an observation into a diagnosis without the required clinical interpretation.

## Example

Weak: `Bad wound; probably infected.`

Stronger: `Observed findings documented; possible inflammatory/infectious burden considered; supporting and missing evidence identified; next assessment and communication recorded.`

## Key takeaway

Uncertainty is not a documentation failure when it is explicit, justified, and paired with a plan to resolve it.
