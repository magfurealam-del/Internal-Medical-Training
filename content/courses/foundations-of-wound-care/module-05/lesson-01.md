# Whole-Patient Assessment Before Wound Therapy

## Learning objectives

- List the components of a full patient assessment and explain why social history belongs in a wound-care assessment.
- Interpret baseline vital signs (blood pressure, pulse, respiratory rate/oxygen saturation, temperature) for signs that could delay wound healing.
- Apply the VIP approach (Vascular, Infection/Inflammation, Pressure) as the first safety screen before any local wound treatment decision.

## Core concepts

Accurate patient and wound assessment is crucial for effective treatment planning: it establishes the baseline used to monitor progress and reveals the underlying causes and barriers to healing before any product or dressing decision is made. Assessment has two parallel tracks that converge into one treatment plan: **patient assessment** and **wound assessment**.

**Patient assessment** starts with the presenting complaint and a brief history of it, then a full medical history (chronic diseases, medications, prior surgeries, age, body type, nutritional status), then **social history** — socioeconomic status, access to care, family support, and lifestyle factors all measurably affect healing capacity and are not optional context.

**Baseline observations (vital signs)** and what they can signal for wound care specifically:

- **Blood pressure** — high or low can indicate circulatory compromise that impacts healing.
- **Pulse** — tachycardia may suggest infection or dehydration; bradycardia could indicate hypothyroidism or heart block.
- **Respiratory rate & oxygen saturation** — both reflect perfusion and overall health.
- **Temperature** — fever or hypothermia may indicate infection or systemic illness.

## Practical application

**The VIP approach** is the first safety screen applied before any local wound therapy is chosen — confirm these three before deciding what to put on the wound:

- **V — Vascular**: is there adequate blood supply to heal, and is compression or aggressive debridement safe? (Screen before compression therapy or advanced biologics.)
- **I — Infection/Inflammation**: is the wound clinically infected or just colonized, and does the inflammatory phase look prolonged/stuck?
- **P — Pressure**: is there an unaddressed pressure or shear source that will keep re-injuring the wound regardless of dressing choice?

Skipping any one of these before choosing a therapy is the single most common way "product-first" decision-making goes wrong — a dressing cannot compensate for unaddressed ischemia, uncontrolled infection, or ongoing pressure.

**Wound assessment itself** covers: type (acute vs. chronic), location (often a clue to cause — e.g. pressure ulcers on heels), and size/depth (measured consistently to track progress over time). The **wound bed** is assessed for tissue type (necrotic/sloughy/granulation/epithelial — see Module 4) and exudate (amount and type, since excess exudate risks maceration of periwound skin). **Infection and surrounding skin** are checked together: erythema, swelling, pain, and increased exudate signal possible infection; the periwound skin is checked separately for maceration, redness, or allergic reaction to dressings, since periwound breakdown can delay healing on its own.

## Key takeaways

- Patient assessment and wound assessment run in parallel and converge into one treatment plan — a wound is never assessed in isolation from the patient it belongs to.
- Social history (access to care, support, lifestyle) is a genuine clinical input to expected healing time, not an administrative afterthought.
- Vital signs are read for wound-relevant signals: BP for circulatory compromise, pulse for infection/dehydration or cardiac issues, temperature for systemic infection.
- VIP (Vascular, Infection/Inflammation, Pressure) is the mandatory safety screen before any local therapy decision — confirm all three before selecting a dressing or device.
