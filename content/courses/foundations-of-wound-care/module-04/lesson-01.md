# Wound Types and Classification by Etiology

## Learning objectives

- Distinguish acute wounds from chronic wounds and state the typical time threshold used to separate them.
- Classify a wound by cause (etiology) among the four major chronic wound types: diabetic foot ulcer, venous leg ulcer, pressure ulcer, and arterial ulcer.
- Identify the wound-bed tissue types (necrotic, sloughy, granulation, epithelial, and others) and state what each indicates about healing status.

## Core concepts

**Acute vs. chronic** is the first classification decision on every wound:

- **Acute wounds**: occur suddenly (trauma or surgery); follow the normal healing phases; usually heal within 4–6 weeks. Examples: surgical wounds, cuts, burns.
- **Chronic wounds**: fail to heal within the expected time frame (commonly defined as persisting beyond 6 weeks); become "stuck" in one phase of healing, usually due to an underlying condition. Common in patients with diabetes, venous insufficiency, or immobility.

**Classify by etiology before treatment** — the four major chronic wound types, distinguished by cause:

| Type | Cause | Key features |
|---|---|---|
| **Diabetic foot ulcer (DFU)** | Peripheral neuropathy (loss of sensation → unnoticed injury) + poor circulation (delayed healing) + pressure/trauma | Often painless due to neuropathy; plantar surface of foot; high infection/amputation risk if untreated |
| **Venous leg ulcer (VLU)** | Venous insufficiency — blood pools in the lower legs, damaging tissue under pressure | Lower legs/ankles; hemosiderin staining (darkened skin); often weepy with heavy exudate |
| **Pressure ulcer (bedsore)** | Prolonged pressure on bony prominences (heels, hips, sacrum), usually in immobile patients | Staged 1–4 (see Module 6 for staging systems) |
| **Arterial ulcer** | Arterial insufficiency (atherosclerosis, PAD) — reduced blood flow to extremities | Toes/feet/ankles; "punched-out" appearance with well-defined borders; cool, pale/bluish skin; pain worse at night |

Acute wound sub-types worth distinguishing on sight: **incised** (clean, sharp-object cuts, straight edges, minimal tissue damage), **lacerated** (blunt-force tears, jagged edges, more tissue damage, may need debridement), **puncture** (small entry, deep track, high infection risk — monitor for tetanus), **bite wounds** (high infection risk from oral bacteria; do not close puncture-type bite wounds — allow drainage), and **skin tears** (common in elderly/fragile skin; classified by the **ISTAP system**: Type 1 = no skin loss, flap repositionable; Type 2 = partial flap loss; Type 3 = total flap loss, entire wound bed exposed).

## Practical application

**Classify the wound bed by tissue type** — this tells you what stage of healing (or failure) you're looking at:

- **Necrotic (black)** — dead tissue; blocks new tissue growth; requires debridement.
- **Sloughy (yellow)** — soft, moist dead cells and debris; hinders healing; needs removal (autolytic or sharp debridement) to allow granulation.
- **Granulation (red)** — healthy, new connective tissue and blood vessels; indicates active healing; protect it, don't disrupt it during dressing changes.
- **Epithelial (pink)** — thin new skin covering the surface; the final healing phase; protect with non-adherent dressings.
- **Hypergranulation** — excess granulation tissue rising above the wound bed; manage with pressure (foam/hydrocolloid) or, in some cases, chemical cauterization.
- **Eschar** — thick, dry, leathery, black/brown necrotic tissue, often firmly attached; requires debridement unless dry, stable, and uninfected, in which case it may be protected rather than removed (this decision depends on vascular status — see Course 3).
- **Fungating tissue** — irregular, raised tissue seen in cancer-related wounds, often with odor/bleeding; avoid aggressive debridement; focus on odor/exudate control.
- **Infected tissue** — red, swollen, warm, exuding pus or foul-smelling drainage; needs antimicrobial therapy and often debridement to reduce bacterial load.

**Factors that impede healing**, in three groups: **intrinsic** (age-related skin changes, diabetes/vascular disease, nutritional deficiency), **extrinsic** (infection, poor wound-care technique, medications like steroids or chemotherapy), and **lifestyle** (smoking, alcohol use, immobility). Any chronic wound assessment should screen across all three, not just the wound bed itself.

## Key takeaways

- Acute vs. chronic is a time-and-trajectory judgment (typically the 6-week threshold), not just a description of appearance.
- The four major chronic wound types (DFU, VLU, pressure ulcer, arterial ulcer) are distinguished primarily by cause, not just location — classify by etiology first.
- Wound-bed tissue color (black/yellow/red/pink) is a direct readout of healing stage: necrotic and sloughy tissue must go; granulation and epithelial tissue must be protected.
- Healing barriers fall into intrinsic, extrinsic, and lifestyle categories — a full classification includes all three, not the wound bed alone.
