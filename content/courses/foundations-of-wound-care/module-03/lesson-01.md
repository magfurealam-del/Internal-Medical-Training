# Wound Healing Biology: Why Wounds Fail

## Learning objectives

- Name the four phases of wound healing in order and describe what happens in each.
- Distinguish healing by primary, secondary, and tertiary intention.
- Explain why chronic wounds stall, and identify the role of the skin microbiome in that process.

## Core concepts

Wound healing is a complex process with **four phases that must occur in sequence and at the right intensity**:

1. **Hemostasis** — begins immediately after injury. Vasoconstriction minimizes blood loss; platelets activate and form a clot, which both stops bleeding and acts as a barrier to bacteria and a scaffold for the cells that will drive healing.
2. **Inflammation** — the vascular/cellular response to injury. Mediators such as prostaglandin and histamine increase blood flow; neutrophils and macrophages remove bacteria and dead tissue. Signs: heat, swelling, redness, pain.
3. **Proliferation** — begins around day 3, lasts up to 14 days. Angiogenesis (new blood vessel formation) is stimulated by growth factors; granulation tissue forms, rich in nutrients and oxygen; wound contraction pulls the edges together.
4. **Maturation** — can last up to a year. Collagen fibers mature and reorganize, increasing tensile strength; the scar becomes less vascular and fades; final scar tissue reaches about 80% of normal skin's strength.

Acute wounds follow this sequence without complication. **Chronic wounds become "stuck" in one phase — usually inflammation — and this is precisely what "why wounds fail" means clinically**: not a different process, but this same process failing to progress.

## Practical application

**Healing by intention** — the closure strategy depends on tissue loss:

- **Primary intention**: edges brought together by sutures, staples, or glue; minimal tissue loss (typical surgical wounds); faster healing, minimal scarring.
- **Secondary intention**: tissue loss is extensive, edges cannot be brought together; slower, more scarring; typical of pressure ulcers and large open wounds.
- **Tertiary intention**: used for contaminated wounds or wounds with excessive exudate — left open initially to drain, closed surgically later; higher infection/tissue-loss risk during the open period.

**Moist wound healing** (George Winter, 1962) is the optimal environment: a moist wound bed heals faster, reduces scarring, and supports autolytic debridement — but excess moisture causes maceration, so balance is the goal (this is the concept behind the MOIST framework covered later in this course).

**The skin microbiome matters to healing outcomes.** Normal flora (e.g. *Staphylococcus epidermidis*, *Corynebacterium*) helps maintain skin health, regulates immune response, and aids healing. Pathogens (e.g. *Staphylococcus aureus*, *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*) can invade, disrupt healing, and drive biofilm formation, making chronic infections harder to treat. Disruption of this balance — from harsh antiseptics, chronic moisture, or antibiotic use — is itself a healing-failure mechanism, not just a side effect.

## Key takeaways

- The four phases (hemostasis → inflammation → proliferation → maturation) must occur in sequence; a "non-healing" chronic wound is a wound stuck in one phase, not a wound outside the process entirely.
- Primary/secondary/tertiary intention describes the closure strategy dictated by how much tissue was lost — this determines expected healing time and scarring, not just technique preference.
- Moist wound healing balances hydration against maceration risk — too dry delays healing, too wet damages periwound skin.
- A healthy skin microbiome is part of normal healing; disrupting it (overuse of antiseptics, chronic excess moisture) can itself become a barrier to healing.
