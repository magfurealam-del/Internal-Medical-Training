# The Four Phases of Wound Healing

## Learning objectives

- Name and sequence the four phases of wound healing
- Explain why each phase must occur in the correct order and at the correct intensity
- Distinguish between how acute and chronic wounds progress through these phases

## Core concepts

Wound healing is not a single event — it is an orderly biological programme that unfolds in four overlapping phases. For a wound to close successfully, each phase must begin at the right time, proceed with the right intensity, and hand off cleanly to the next.

### The four phases in sequence

| Phase | When it begins | Core activity |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Hemostasis | Immediately after injury | Stop bleeding; seal the wound |
| 2. Inflammation | Minutes to hours | Remove pathogens and debris; signal repair |
| 3. Proliferation | Day 3 onwards (up to 14 days) | Rebuild tissue; close the wound |
| 4. Maturation / Remodelling | Weeks to up to 1 year | Strengthen the repair |

These phases are not mutually exclusive — inflammation and proliferation overlap, and maturation begins while proliferation is still finishing. What matters is the overall direction of travel: forward through the sequence.

### Acute versus chronic wounds

An **acute wound** — such as a clean surgical incision or a straightforward laceration — follows the four-phase sequence without major interruption. Healing is predictable and time-limited.

A **chronic wound** — such as a pressure injury, a diabetic foot ulcer, or a venous leg ulcer — becomes stalled, most often in the inflammatory phase. Instead of resolving and moving into proliferation, the wound persists in a destructive, high-protease environment that breaks down growth factors and new tissue as fast as the body can produce them.

Understanding **which phase a wound is stuck in** is the first step in deciding what intervention will move it forward.

### Why sequence and intensity both matter

Too little of a phase causes problems. If hemostasis fails, blood loss continues and the clot scaffold is not established. If inflammation is blunted by immunosuppression, the wound bed remains contaminated and proliferation cannot begin on a clean surface.

Too much of a phase is equally harmful. Excessive or prolonged inflammation produces large amounts of matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) that destroy the extracellular matrix and growth factors the wound needs. Chronic inflammation is why chronic wounds look wet, sloughy, and fail to develop healthy granulation tissue.

## Practical application

When you assess a wound, ask: which phase should this wound be in given its age, and is it actually there?

- A surgical wound on day 10 that is still hot, swollen, and producing copious exudate may be stuck in inflammation — suspect infection or biofilm.
- A wound with pale, fragile granulation that does not contract over weeks may have a proliferation problem — suspect poor perfusion or nutritional deficit.
- A healed wound that re-opens may have failed maturation — collagen remodelling was insufficient.

This framework drives every clinical decision that follows in this course.

## Key takeaways

- Wound healing has four phases: hemostasis, inflammation, proliferation, maturation.
- Phases must occur in sequence and at optimal intensity.
- Acute wounds progress predictably; chronic wounds stall — most often in inflammation.
- Identifying the stalled phase guides treatment selection.
