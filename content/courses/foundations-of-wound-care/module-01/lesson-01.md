# Mechanism-First Wound Care: How This Bible Works

## Learning objectives

- Explain the mechanism-first training sequence: barrier → science → assessment → therapy → contraindication → monitoring.
- State why product-first decision-making is avoided in Ekagra training.
- Use the Master Barrier-to-Therapy Map to trace a clinical finding back to its underlying biological barrier before naming a product or device.

## Core concepts

Ekagra's internal wound care training is built on one core training sentence: **mechanism first, product second, measurement always.** Every topic in this Bible is taught in the same order: identify the barrier, understand the biology behind it, assess the patient, match a therapy to the mechanism, screen for contraindications, and monitor the outcome.

This module deliberately avoids:

- Bedside-manner or generic communication scripting.
- Sales claims that are not supported by mechanism or indication.
- Product-first decision-making.
- Treatment without contraindication screening.
- Advanced therapy without objective monitoring.

## Practical application

The **Master Barrier-to-Therapy Map** is the one-page logic map every later disease pathway and product page traces back to. Each row links a biological barrier to its diagnostic focus and its therapy focus:

| Barrier | Diagnostic focus | Therapy focus |
|---|---|---|
| Poor perfusion | Pulse exam, capillary refill, limb temperature comparison | Vascular referral and revascularization planning; protective wound care while ischemia is clarified |
| Low tissue oxygenation | Mimosa tissue oxygenation and thermal imaging; vascular exam | HBOT for selected hypoxic wounds; perfusion optimization and vascular referral |
| Biofilm and recurrent slough | Percentage of slough/necrosis, depth and undermining | Structured wound hygiene; sharp or conservative debridement when safe |
| Clinical infection and inflammatory burden | Local vs. spreading severity, systemic symptoms and vitals | Cleansing and debridement when safe; topical antimicrobial dressings for local burden |
| Pressure, shear, repetitive trauma | Wound location and load exposure; footwear and walking pattern | Total contact cast/removable walker where available; pneumatic boot, crutches, or wheelchair when needed |
| Edema, venous hypertension, lymphatic failure | Pitting vs. non-pitting edema; limb circumference | Compression after vascular screening; Airos/pneumatic compression for selected lymphedema/edema |
| Neuropathy and loss of protective sensation | Monofilament/vibration screening; NeuroTouch sensory screening | Protective footwear and offloading; callus management |
| Moisture imbalance and periwound breakdown | Exudate amount, color, viscosity, odor; dressing wear time | Absorbent foam or superabsorbent dressing; gelling fiber/alginate when appropriate |
| Necrosis, eschar, tissue non-viability | Dry vs. wet stability; vascular status | Protect dry stable ischemic eschar pending vascular decision; urgent debridement/surgery review for wet/infected necrosis |
| Wound edge failure (epibole, callus, undermining, non-advancement) | Edge type and undermining depth; callus and pressure source | Callus reduction if safe; edge refashioning/debridement by a trained clinician |

**Doctor knowledge expectation:** explain the pathophysiology behind the topic, identify when a wound is unsafe for local-only care, select or approve advanced therapy only once the barrier is defined, and recognize when vascular, surgical, infectious disease, or hospital escalation is needed.

**Nurse knowledge expectation:** recognize clinical clues at dressing change or assessment, document wound measurements and tissue changes consistently, alert the physician when red flags or non-healing patterns appear, and support offloading, compression, device sessions, and dressing logic according to the care plan.

## Key takeaways

- The training order is always barrier → science → assessment → therapy → contraindication → monitoring — never product first.
- The Master Barrier-to-Therapy Map is the reference point every later module's disease- and device-specific content traces back to.
- Doctors and nurses have distinct but linked responsibilities: doctors define the barrier and approve therapy; nurses recognize clues, document, and escalate.
