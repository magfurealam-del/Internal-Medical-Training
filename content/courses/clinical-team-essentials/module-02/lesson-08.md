# Chronic Wounds and the Skin Microbiome

## Learning objectives

- Define a chronic wound and explain what makes it different from an acute wound
- Identify the common types of chronic wound seen in clinical practice
- Describe the composition and function of the skin microbiome
- Explain how disruption of microbial balance contributes to chronic wound development and biofilm

## Core concepts

### Chronic wounds: when healing stalls

A **chronic wound** is defined as a wound that has failed to proceed through the normal phases of healing in an orderly and timely manner — typically one that has not healed within 4–6 weeks despite appropriate management.

Chronic wounds are not simply "slow" acute wounds. They exist in a fundamentally different biological state:

| Feature | Acute wound | Chronic wound |
|---|---|---|
| Inflammatory phase | Resolves in 3–5 days | Persists for weeks to months |
| Exudate | Moderate, serous | High-volume, often cloudy or purulent |
| Protease activity | Normal and controlled | Excessive — MMPs destroy growth factors |
| Growth factors | Present in adequate amounts | Degraded by excess proteases |
| Bacterial load | Controlled or absent | High, often including biofilm |
| Wound edges | Advancing keratinocytes | Rolled, non-advancing edges ("epibole") |

### Common chronic wound types

The most frequently encountered chronic wounds in clinical practice are:

1. **Pressure injuries (pressure ulcers)** — caused by sustained mechanical compression reducing tissue perfusion, typically over bony prominences
2. **Diabetic foot ulcers (DFUs)** — caused by a combination of neuropathy (removing protective sensation), vasculopathy (reducing perfusion), and mechanical stress
3. **Venous leg ulcers (VLUs)** — caused by chronic venous hypertension, typically in the gaiter region
4. **Arterial/ischaemic ulcers** — caused by peripheral arterial disease and insufficient tissue oxygenation
5. **Mixed aetiology ulcers** — combining elements of venous insufficiency and arterial disease

Each type has a different dominant pathological mechanism. Accurate identification of aetiology is essential before treatment is selected — a compression bandage that would heal a venous leg ulcer could cause irreversible ischaemia in an arterial ulcer.

### The skin microbiome

The **skin microbiome** is the diverse community of microorganisms — bacteria, fungi, viruses, and mites — that colonise healthy skin. Far from being purely pathogenic, this community performs essential functions:

- **Competitive exclusion**: commensal organisms occupy attachment sites and consume nutrients, preventing opportunistic pathogens from establishing themselves
- **Immune regulation**: commensals interact with skin immune cells (Langerhans cells, dermal dendritic cells) to maintain appropriate immune tone
- **Barrier reinforcement**: certain commensal species produce antimicrobial peptides and fatty acids that inhibit pathogen growth

Key commensal organisms include *Staphylococcus epidermidis*, *Corynebacterium* spp., and *Cutibacterium acnes*. These are not harmful — they are active participants in skin defence.

### Disruption of the microbiome and wound infection

When skin integrity is lost (as in any wound), the microbiome changes. The protective barrier is breached and:

1. **Contamination** — organisms enter the wound from the environment; all open wounds are contaminated
2. **Colonisation** — organisms establish themselves in the wound; the host immune response is managing them without tissue damage
3. **Critical colonisation** — bacterial burden increases to a level that begins to delay healing; the host response is insufficient to maintain control
4. **Infection** — organisms invade tissue, overwhelming host defences; systemic signs (fever, elevated CRP) may develop

This is the **contamination–colonisation–critical colonisation–infection** continuum. Most chronic wounds sit in the critical colonisation zone — they are not clinically infected, but the bacterial load is high enough to perpetuate inflammation and prevent progression to proliferation.

### Biofilm: the chronic wound's most common barrier

**Biofilm** is a structured community of bacteria encased in a self-produced polysaccharide matrix (glycocalyx). In a biofilm:

- Bacteria are up to **1,000 times more resistant** to antibiotics than planktonic (free-floating) bacteria
- The immune system cannot access bacteria effectively through the matrix
- Biofilm perpetuates chronic inflammation and MMP over-production

Biofilm cannot be resolved by antibiotic therapy alone. It requires **physical disruption** (debridement, mechanical cleansing) combined with antimicrobial agents that penetrate the matrix. This is a core principle of wound bed preparation and will be addressed in the TIME module.

## Practical application

- If a wound has been present for more than 4–6 weeks without meaningful progress, consider whether the underlying aetiology has been identified and addressed — not whether a new dressing is needed.
- Assess for critical colonisation signs: stalled healing, malodour, increased exudate, fragile or friable granulation — even in the absence of systemic infection signs.
- Do not rely on wound swabs alone to diagnose biofilm — biofilm organisms often cannot be cultured with standard techniques.
- Wound bed preparation (debridement + appropriate antimicrobials + moisture balance + edge management) is the structured approach to breaking the chronic wound cycle.

## Key takeaways

- Chronic wounds are biologically distinct from acute wounds — they are stalled in a destructive inflammatory environment, not simply slow to heal.
- The most common chronic wound types are pressure injuries, diabetic foot ulcers, and venous leg ulcers.
- The skin microbiome is a protective community; disruption promotes pathogen overgrowth.
- The contamination → colonisation → critical colonisation → infection continuum guides assessment.
- Biofilm is a critical barrier in chronic wounds: it is resistant to antibiotics and requires physical debridement.
