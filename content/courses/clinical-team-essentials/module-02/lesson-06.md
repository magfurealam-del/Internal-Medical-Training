# Types of Wound Healing Intention

## Learning objectives

- Define healing by primary, secondary, and tertiary intention
- Identify the clinical situations in which each type applies
- Explain the implications of each healing type for time, scarring, and infection risk

## Core concepts

Wounds do not all heal in the same way. The path a wound takes from injury to closure depends on how much tissue has been lost, how clean the wound is, and how the clinician chooses to manage it. The three categories — primary, secondary, and tertiary intention — describe this pathway.

### Healing by primary intention

**Definition**: The wound edges are brought together mechanically, eliminating the cavity between them.

**How it is achieved**:
- Sutures (continuous or interrupted)
- Staples
- Tissue adhesive (glue)
- Wound closure strips (Steri-Strips)

**Characteristics**:
- Minimal tissue loss — the wound is clean and the edges can be approximated without tension
- Healing is fast — the wound closes primarily by epithelialisation across a very narrow gap
- Scarring is minimal — the narrow approximated edges produce a fine linear scar
- Infection risk is low when the wound is clean and closure is prompt

**Typical examples**: surgical incisions, clean lacerations within 6–8 hours of injury (before bacterial contamination becomes established), selected traumatic wounds after thorough irrigation and debridement.

**Clinical principle**: for primary closure to succeed, the wound must be clean (or cleanable), adequately perfused, and the edges must approximate without tension.

### Healing by secondary intention

**Definition**: The wound is left open and heals through the natural proliferative and maturation processes — granulation, contraction, and epithelialisation — without surgical closure.

**When it is used**:
- Tissue loss is too great for edges to be approximated
- The wound is infected or heavily contaminated
- The wound geometry makes closure impractical (e.g. cavity wounds, pressure injuries)

**Characteristics**:
- Slower to heal — the entire wound cavity must fill with granulation tissue before epithelialisation can complete
- More prone to scarring — a wider, irregular scar forms
- Requires active wound management — dressings, moisture balance, and regular assessment throughout
- Wound contraction plays a larger role in reducing wound size than in primary intention

**Typical examples**: pressure injuries, diabetic foot ulcers, pilonidal abscesses after surgical drainage, infected traumatic wounds.

### Healing by tertiary intention (delayed primary closure)

**Definition**: The wound is initially left open — to allow drainage, debridement, or antibiotic treatment — and is then closed surgically at a later stage once the wound is deemed clean.

**When it is used**:
- Contaminated wounds where primary closure carries an unacceptably high infection risk
- Wounds with excessive exudate or necrotic tissue at the time of initial presentation
- War wounds, highly contaminated traumatic injuries, and some abdominal surgical wounds

**Characteristics**:
- Higher infection risk than primary intention during the open period
- Risk of tissue loss increases the longer the wound remains open
- Healing after secondary closure proceeds similarly to primary intention, but with a higher rate of complications

**Typical examples**: heavily contaminated traumatic wounds managed with initial irrigation and delayed closure, laparostomy wounds closed after 48–72 hours.

### Comparing the three types

| Feature | Primary | Secondary | Tertiary |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tissue loss | Minimal | Significant | Variable |
| Wound edges | Approximated | Open | Open then closed |
| Healing time | Days to weeks | Weeks to months | Variable |
| Scarring | Minimal, linear | More extensive | Between the two |
| Infection risk | Low if clean | Moderate | Higher during open phase |

## Practical application

- When assessing a wound for suitability of closure, consider: How long since injury? How contaminated? What is the perfusion status? — these determine whether primary closure is safe.
- A wound that was closed primarily but breaks down should be reassessed: it may need to heal by secondary intention or undergo re-debridement and delayed closure.
- Pressure injuries, diabetic foot ulcers, and venous leg ulcers almost always heal by secondary intention — plan care accordingly (dressings, moisture management, offloading).

## Key takeaways

- Primary intention: edges brought together, minimal tissue loss, fast healing, minimal scarring.
- Secondary intention: open wound heals via granulation and contraction — slower, more scarring.
- Tertiary intention: initially open for drainage/debridement, then surgically closed later.
- The choice of healing pathway depends on wound contamination, tissue loss, and perfusion.
