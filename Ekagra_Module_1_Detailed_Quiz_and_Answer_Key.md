# Course 1 — Module 1 Quiz
## Skin Anatomy and Physiology

**Assessment type:** Single-best-answer MCQ only  
**Questions:** 20  
**Pass threshold:** 80%  
**Recommended pass score:** 16/20  
**Source basis:** Ekagra Course 1, Theoretical Module 1, Slides 1–8; related wound-assessment teaching materials

## Instructions

Choose the one best answer. The aim is to test whether the learner can connect anatomy to mechanism, clinical findings, assessment, and documentation.

### Q1 — Anatomy as clinical reasoning

Which statement best explains why skin anatomy is foundational to wound care?

A. It allows the clinician to memorize dressing brand names.
B. It identifies which structures and functions have been lost and therefore which mechanisms must be assessed.
C. It proves the wound is infected whenever the epidermis is open.
D. It replaces the need for patient and wound assessment.

**Answer:** B  
**Source:** Vault Module 1, Slide 1  
**Rationale:** The layer and structures involved predict barrier loss, tissue strength, sensation, perfusion, depth, and repair capacity. Anatomy supports mechanism-based assessment; it does not replace it.

### Q2 — Layer-function matching

Which pairing is most accurate?

A. Epidermis — primary source of deep tensile strength and large vessels
B. Dermis — connective-tissue support containing vessels, nerves, and appendages
C. Subcutaneous tissue — only a superficial waterproof barrier
D. Epidermis — main storage site for adipose energy reserves

**Answer:** B  
**Source:** Vault Module 1, Slides 1, 4, and 5  
**Rationale:** The dermis provides connective-tissue support and contains vessels, nerves, glands, and follicles. The other choices assign functions to the wrong layer.

### Q3 — Epidermal cell function

Which epidermal cell is primarily responsible for producing keratin and forming most of the physical surface barrier?

A. Keratinocyte
B. Melanocyte
C. Langerhans cell
D. Merkel cell

**Answer:** A  
**Source:** Vault Module 1, Slide 2  
**Rationale:** Keratinocytes are the dominant epidermal cells and produce keratin. Melanocytes contribute melanin; Langerhans cells participate in immune surveillance; Merkel cells contribute to touch sensation.

### Q4 — Epidermal renewal

Why can a superficial epidermal injury sometimes repair more readily than a deep dermal injury?

A. The epidermis has no cells capable of renewal.
B. Epidermal cells can proliferate and migrate from viable margins or appendage-associated sources when those structures remain intact.
C. The dermis is not involved in any wound-healing process.
D. Superficial wounds never become chronic.

**Answer:** B  
**Source:** Vault Module 1, Slides 2–3; related skin-integrity teaching  
**Rationale:** Renewal and migration depend on viable cells and an adequate environment. Deeper injury removes more structural support and may destroy appendage-associated epithelial sources.

### Q5 — Barrier failure

A patient has fissures, excessive dryness, and increasing irritation around an otherwise shallow wound. Which mechanism should be considered first?

A. Improved epidermal barrier function
B. Increased transepidermal water loss and impaired surface protection
C. Deep bone infection proven by the fissures
D. Normal maturation of scar tissue

**Answer:** B  
**Source:** Vault Module 1, Slides 2–3  
**Rationale:** Fissuring and dryness suggest surface-barrier disruption, which can increase water loss and external access. They do not by themselves prove deep infection.

### Q6 — Dermal matrix

Which dermal components most directly contribute to tensile strength and elastic recoil?

A. Collagen and elastin
B. Melanin and keratin only
C. Platelets and erythrocytes only
D. Sweat and sebum only

**Answer:** A  
**Source:** Vault Module 1, Slide 4 and “Layers of the Dermis”  
**Rationale:** Collagen resists tension and elastin supports recoil. Their organization and remodeling influence tissue strength.

### Q7 — Dermal injury interpretation

A wound appears clean on the surface but the surrounding tissue is weak and easily stretched. Which interpretation is most appropriate?

A. Surface cleanliness proves that dermal structure is normal.
B. Dermal matrix injury or reduced structural support may persist despite a clean surface.
C. The patient cannot have a chronic wound because there is no slough.
D. The only relevant finding is wound length.

**Answer:** B  
**Source:** Vault Module 1, Slide 4  
**Rationale:** Surface appearance does not fully describe dermal strength, elasticity, vascular support, or remodeling status.

### Q8 — Sensation and protection

A patient repeatedly injures a foot but reports little pain. What mechanism should the team investigate?

A. Increased protective sensation
B. Loss of protective sensation allowing unnoticed repetitive trauma
C. Excessive epidermal renewal
D. Normal dermal elasticity

**Answer:** B  
**Source:** Vault Module 1, Slides 4 and 6; supplementary diabetic-foot guide, PDF pp. 6–8  
**Rationale:** Reduced sensation removes an early warning system. The patient may continue loading an injured area without the expected pain signal.

### Q9 — Subcutaneous tissue

Which is the best explanation for why loss of subcutaneous cushioning can increase pressure injury risk?

A. It increases the distance between skin and bone.
B. It can bring the skin closer to deeper structures and concentrate force over a smaller area.
C. It permanently eliminates all external pressure.
D. It makes perfusion assessment unnecessary.

**Answer:** B  
**Source:** Vault Module 1, Slide 5  
**Rationale:** Subcutaneous tissue cushions and distributes force. Loss of padding can increase local stress and tissue vulnerability.

### Q10 — Shear mechanism

Which situation most specifically suggests shear rather than simple surface friction?

A. A superficial dry scale with no movement between layers
B. Sliding of the body while the skin remains relatively fixed, producing stress between tissue layers
C. Normal sweating during exercise
D. A healed scar with no mechanical exposure

**Answer:** B  
**Source:** Vault Module 1, Slide 5; related wound-assessment teaching  
**Rationale:** Shear creates differential movement between tissue layers and can injure deeper microvascular structures even when the surface initially looks limited.

### Q11 — Skin functions

Which finding most directly demonstrates loss of the skin’s protective sensory function?

A. A patient withdraws immediately from a painful stimulus.
B. A patient continues walking on a high-pressure area without recognizing injury.
C. A patient develops normal sweating in heat.
D. A patient has intact color and temperature.

**Answer:** B  
**Source:** Vault Module 1, Slide 6  
**Rationale:** Protective sensation helps the person detect and avoid injury. Continued loading without awareness suggests sensory impairment.

### Q12 — Temperature interpretation

A foot is cooler and paler than the opposite side. Which is the most appropriate interpretation?

A. Perfusion alteration should be considered and assessed; the finding alone does not establish a diagnosis.
B. The patient definitely has infection.
C. The difference is irrelevant if the wound is small.
D. The finding proves the epidermis is infected.

**Answer:** A  
**Source:** Vault Module 1, Slide 6; related vascular-assessment teaching  
**Rationale:** Temperature and color can provide perfusion clues. They require contextual assessment rather than an automatic diagnosis.

### Q13 — Aging skin

Why can older skin be injured by a stress that younger skin tolerates?

A. Aging always increases dermal thickness and elasticity.
B. Aging can reduce structural resilience, cushioning, vascular reserve, and repair margin.
C. Aging eliminates the need for pressure prevention.
D. Aging makes skin immune to adhesive trauma.

**Answer:** B  
**Source:** Vault Module 1, Slide 7  
**Rationale:** Age-related changes reduce the margin between applied stress and tissue failure. They increase vulnerability but do not determine the cause of every injury.

### Q14 — Fragile skin as a mechanism question

An older patient develops a tear during adhesive removal. Which assessment is most complete?

A. Record only “fragile skin.”
B. Assess dryness, dermal vulnerability, edema, adhesive exposure, handling, medication context, mobility, and the exact injury.
C. Assume infection is the only cause.
D. Change the dressing without documenting the event.

**Answer:** B  
**Source:** Vault Module 1, Slide 7; related skin-integrity materials  
**Rationale:** “Fragile” is a risk description, not a complete mechanism. The team should identify both vulnerability and applied stress.

### Q15 — Diabetes and mechanism

Why should a diabetic-foot assessment include sensation, perfusion, footwear, and loading rather than wound appearance alone?

A. Diabetes affects only the epidermis.
B. Diabetes can alter sensation, perfusion, immune response, tissue repair, and mechanical exposure simultaneously.
C. Footwear has no relationship to pressure.
D. A painless wound is always low risk.

**Answer:** B  
**Source:** Vault Module 1, Slide 7; supplementary diabetic-foot guide, PDF pp. 6–8  
**Rationale:** Diabetic foot risk is multifactorial. A painless lesion can be high risk when protective sensation is impaired.

### Q16 — Depth assessment

Which sequence best supports an anatomy-based wound assessment?

A. Product name → dressing color → wound age → patient history
B. Surface barrier → dermal support → subcutaneous cushioning → deeper structures
C. Pain score only → wound length only → treatment
D. Odor only → dressing choice → diagnosis

**Answer:** B  
**Source:** Vault Module 1, Slides 1–8; wound-assessment teaching  
**Rationale:** Layered assessment links visible findings to lost structures and functions. It must be combined with etiology, perfusion, sensation, pressure, infection, and systemic context.

### Q17 — Documentation quality

Which entry is most useful for clinical communication and audit?

A. “Large bad ulcer.”
B. “Skin problem; dressing applied.”
C. “Left plantar wound; surface, visible tissue, depth/undermining status, periwound, sensation, perfusion clues, loading context, suspected mechanism, and monitoring plan documented.”
D. “Patient tolerated.”

**Answer:** C  
**Source:** Vault Module 1, Slides 1–8; Module 4 wound-assessment teaching  
**Rationale:** An auditable record connects anatomy and findings to mechanism, assessment, action, and monitoring.

### Q18 — Surface versus deeper mechanism

A shallow wound has a wet, white, painful periwound. Which mechanism should be assessed?

A. Moisture imbalance causing epidermal barrier breakdown
B. Normal subcutaneous cushioning
C. Improved epithelial maturation
D. Normal dry scar formation

**Answer:** A  
**Source:** Vault Module 1, Slides 2 and 6; related periwound teaching  
**Rationale:** White, soft, painful surrounding skin suggests moisture-related barrier injury that may worsen the wound environment.

### Q19 — Integrated mechanism

A plantar wound has callus, worn footwear, reduced sensation, and weak pulses. Which is the best first clinical reasoning statement?

A. The wound is caused by callus alone.
B. Multiple mechanisms may coexist: repetitive pressure, sensory loss, footwear-related loading, and possible perfusion limitation; these require structured assessment.
C. The patient should receive the strongest available device immediately.
D. Weak pulses are irrelevant if the wound is painless.

**Answer:** B  
**Source:** Vault Module 1 integrated teaching; supplementary diabetic-foot guide, PDF pp. 6–8  
**Rationale:** The case contains interacting mechanical, neurologic, footwear, and vascular clues. Course 1 requires mechanism recognition and assessment before therapy selection.

### Q20 — Professor-level synthesis

Which statement best summarizes the relationship between anatomy and wound care?

A. Anatomy describes the wound but does not influence treatment reasoning.
B. Each injured layer represents lost functions; those losses create mechanisms that determine assessment priorities, safety concerns, and the conditions required for repair.
C. Wound size alone predicts healing.
D. If the surface is covered, the underlying mechanism is corrected.

**Answer:** B  
**Source:** Vault Module 1, Slides 1–8; related wound-assessment teaching  
**Rationale:** Anatomy is the bridge from description to mechanism. Coverage may protect a wound environment, but it does not automatically correct pressure, perfusion, sensory, inflammatory, or systemic barriers.
