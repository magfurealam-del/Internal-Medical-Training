# Module 1 — Skin Integrity, Aging, Diabetes, and Fragility

**Vault source:** Course 1, Theoretical Module 1, Slide 7 and related skin-integrity material  
**Primary mechanism:** aging and systemic disease shift the balance between tissue stress and repair capacity.

## Aging changes the margin of safety

With age, skin commonly becomes thinner and less resilient, subcutaneous cushioning may decrease or redistribute, collagen and elastin behavior changes, vascular reserve may be reduced, and repair responses may be slower. These changes do not make injury inevitable; they reduce the amount of stress the tissue can tolerate before breakdown.

## Diabetes changes several systems at once

Diabetes may affect sensation, perfusion, immune response, collagen formation, hydration, and the patient’s ability to perceive or respond to injury. A diabetic foot assessment must therefore be mechanism-based: inspect the skin and footwear, assess sensation and loading, consider perfusion, and review wound trajectory.

## Fragility is a mechanism, not a label

“Fragile skin” should prompt questions:

- Is the epidermal barrier dry, macerated, or inflamed?
- Is the dermis thin or structurally weakened?
- Is there edema or reduced perfusion?
- Is the patient using adhesive products or experiencing traumatic removal?
- Is sensation impaired?
- Is nutrition, mobility, or medication contributing?

### Doctor checkpoint

Separate baseline vulnerability from the active cause of the current lesion. Multiple mechanisms may coexist.

### Nurse checkpoint

Protect vulnerable skin during positioning, cleansing, adhesive use, and dressing removal; document the response and any new skin injury.

## Clinical case

A thin-skinned older patient develops a skin tear after adhesive removal. The visible tear is the event, not the whole explanation. Contributing mechanisms may include reduced dermal strength, adhesive trauma, dryness, poor handling, anticoagulation, edema, or impaired mobility. The assessment must identify which factors are present and modifiable.

## Take-home rule

Risk is the interaction between tissue vulnerability and applied stress. Prevention reduces stress; treatment addresses the injury and the mechanism that created it.
