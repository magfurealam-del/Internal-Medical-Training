# Hemostasis: Stopping the Bleed

## Learning objectives

- Describe the events that occur during the hemostasis phase
- Explain the roles of vasoconstriction and platelet aggregation
- Identify the dual function of the clot as a bleeding barrier and a healing scaffold

## Core concepts

Hemostasis is the body's immediate response to injury. It begins within seconds and its purpose is simple: stop blood loss and seal the wound against the external environment before repair can begin.

### Vasoconstriction — the first second

The moment a blood vessel is damaged, it reflexively constricts. This vasoconstriction is a neurogenic response that reduces blood flow to the injury site within the first 30–60 seconds. It slows blood loss dramatically before any clotting mechanism activates.

### Platelet activation and aggregation

As blood contacts the exposed collagen of the damaged vessel wall, platelets become activated. They:

1. **Adhere** to the exposed collagen via von Willebrand factor
2. **Activate** — changing shape and releasing chemical signals (ADP, thromboxane A₂)
3. **Aggregate** — recruiting additional platelets to form a primary platelet plug

This platelet plug is loose and temporary but sufficient to reduce immediate haemorrhage.

### The fibrin clot

The coagulation cascade converts fibrinogen to fibrin, which cross-links across the platelet plug and creates a stable clot. The clot serves two functions:

- **Immediate barrier**: prevents further blood loss and blocks bacterial entry
- **Provisional matrix**: provides a scaffold of fibrin across which cells — neutrophils, macrophages, and fibroblasts — will migrate during the subsequent phases

If this scaffold is disrupted (by premature removal of eschar, aggressive irrigation, or anticoagulant medication) the foundation for healing is damaged before it has been built.

### Growth factors released at hemostasis

Activated platelets release growth factors that initiate the next phase of healing, including:

- **Platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF)**: recruits neutrophils and macrophages to the wound
- **Transforming growth factor beta (TGF-β)**: stimulates fibroblast proliferation later
- **Vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF)**: primes angiogenesis

Hemostasis is therefore not just a mechanical plug — it is the starting gun for the entire repair programme.

## Practical application

- A wound that re-bleeds easily on contact may have insufficient platelet aggregation — check anticoagulant or antiplatelet therapy.
- Dry, stable eschar overlying a wound in hemostasis (particularly on heels) should not be debrided: it is the clot in its solid form and protects the underlying tissue.
- In acute haemorrhage, direct pressure supports vasoconstriction and platelet aggregation — this is why sustained pressure (not dabbing) controls bleeding.

## Key takeaways

- Vasoconstriction occurs within seconds; it slows blood loss before clotting begins.
- Platelets adhere, activate, and aggregate to form a primary plug.
- The fibrin clot seals the wound and acts as a scaffold for cell migration.
- Platelet-derived growth factors launched from the clot initiate subsequent healing phases.
