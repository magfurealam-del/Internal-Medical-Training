# Maturation: Strengthening and Remodelling

## Learning objectives

- Describe what happens to collagen during the maturation phase
- Explain why healed scar tissue is never as strong as original skin
- Identify the clinical implications of the maturation phase for patient care

## Core concepts

The maturation phase (also called the **remodelling phase**) is the final stage of wound healing. It begins once the wound has closed and continues for up to **12 months** — far longer than any other phase. Its purpose is to convert the provisional repair scaffolding into durable scar tissue with meaningful tensile strength.

### Collagen remodelling

During proliferation, fibroblasts lay down **type III collagen** — a relatively weak, disorganised form arranged in a random basket-weave pattern. During maturation:

1. **Type III collagen** is progressively replaced by **type I collagen** — the stronger, more organised form found in normal dermis
2. Collagen fibres are **cross-linked** by the enzyme lysyl oxidase, increasing tensile strength
3. The fibres reorganise along lines of mechanical stress — this is why scars become less pliable over time

This remodelling is continuous and dynamic: collagenase enzymes break down old fibres at the same time as new fibres are deposited. The net balance tips gradually towards a stronger repair.

### Tensile strength recovery

Despite up to 12 months of remodelling, **scar tissue only recovers approximately 80% of the tensile strength of unwounded skin**. This has important clinical consequences:

- A fully closed wound is still mechanically weaker than surrounding skin
- Wounds over bony prominences, joints, or high-movement areas are at elevated risk of re-opening during the maturation phase
- Pressure offloading and protective strategies should continue well beyond the point of visual closure

### Scar vascularity and appearance

During proliferation, the wound bed is richly vascularised — this is why fresh scars are red and raised. During maturation:

- **Blood vessel density decreases** — the rich capillary network established during angiogenesis is pruned back
- The scar becomes less red and gradually pales to a white or silvery colour
- The scar flattens as excess collagen is remodelled

### Abnormal maturation: hypertrophic and keloid scars

In some patients, the balance between collagen synthesis and breakdown is disrupted:

| Type | Description | Stays within wound margins? | Risk factors |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hypertrophic scar** | Raised, red, firm — excess collagen within wound boundary | Yes | Burns, wounds under tension |
| **Keloid scar** | Raised, extends beyond wound boundary | No (invades surrounding skin) | Genetic predisposition, darker skin tones |

Both represent an imbalance in the remodelling programme and may require specialist management (compression, silicone, intralesional corticosteroids, or surgery).

### Contracture

As myofibroblast activity persists, scars across joints or on the face can shorten and restrict range of motion — this is **scar contracture**. Unlike wound contraction (which is normal and beneficial during healing), contracture causes functional impairment and may require physiotherapy, splinting, or surgical release.

## Practical application

- Do not assume a visually closed wound is fully healed — the patient needs protection from mechanical stress for months.
- Counsel patients that scar redness and firmness are normal and will improve over 6–12 months.
- Patients with keloid or hypertrophic scars from previous injuries should be identified before any elective surgical wound is created — preventive measures can be planned in advance.
- Active scar management (silicone gel, massage, compression) should begin as soon as epithelialisation is complete.

## Key takeaways

- Maturation replaces weak type III collagen with stronger type I collagen, cross-linked and reorganised along stress lines.
- The phase lasts up to 12 months; healed scars only reach ~80% of original skin strength.
- Scar vascularity decreases during maturation — this is why fresh red scars fade over time.
- Hypertrophic and keloid scars represent pathological over-production of collagen during remodelling.
- Closed wounds remain mechanically vulnerable and need protection during remodelling.
