# Inflammation: The Body's Defence Mechanism

## Learning objectives

- Describe the vascular and cellular events of the inflammatory phase
- Identify the key inflammatory mediators and the cells they recruit
- Recognise the clinical signs of inflammation and distinguish normal from pathological inflammation
- Explain what prolonged inflammation does to the wound healing environment

## Core concepts

Inflammation begins within minutes of injury and normally resolves within three to five days in an acute wound. Its purpose is to clean the wound bed — removing bacteria, dead tissue, and cellular debris — so that proliferation can begin on a sterile, prepared surface.

### The vascular response

Immediately after hemostasis, the wound enters an active hyperaemic phase. Injured cells and mast cells release **histamine** and **prostaglandins**, causing:

- **Vasodilation**: blood vessels widen, increasing blood flow to the wound
- **Increased vascular permeability**: plasma proteins and immune cells can leave the capillaries and enter the wound tissue
- **Exudate formation**: the outflow of protein-rich fluid into the wound bed — this fluid contains antibodies, clotting factors, and white blood cells

### The cellular response: neutrophils arrive first

Within the first 24–48 hours, **neutrophils** are the dominant cell type in the wound:

- They engulf and destroy bacteria by phagocytosis
- They release proteolytic enzymes and reactive oxygen species that kill pathogens
- They signal further immune recruitment
- After completing their role, they undergo apoptosis and are cleared by macrophages

### Macrophages: the master regulators

From day 2–3, **macrophages** replace neutrophils as the dominant cell:

- They continue phagocytosis of bacteria and necrotic tissue
- They clear apoptotic neutrophils, reducing the inflammatory load
- They release **growth factors** — PDGF, TGF-β, VEGF — that initiate proliferation
- They orchestrate the transition from the inflammatory to the proliferative phase

Macrophages are not simply scavengers. Without adequate macrophage activity, wounds fail to transition out of inflammation regardless of how much local care is applied.

### Clinical signs of acute inflammation

The four cardinal signs of inflammation are:

| Sign | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Rubor** (redness) | Vasodilation increases blood flow |
| **Calor** (heat) | Increased metabolic activity and blood flow |
| **Tumor** (swelling) | Vascular leakage of plasma into the interstitium |
| **Dolor** (pain) | Prostaglandins and bradykinin stimulate pain receptors |

A fifth sign — **loss of function** — is often added. These signs are normal and expected in the first few days. They become a clinical concern when they persist beyond the expected window or spread beyond the wound margin.

### Normal inflammation versus pathological inflammation

| Feature | Normal (acute) | Pathological (chronic/infected) |
|---|---|---|
| Duration | 3–5 days in acute wounds | Weeks to months |
| Exudate | Serous or serosanguineous, moderate | Purulent, high-volume, offensive |
| Perilesional change | Mild erythema at wound edge | Spreading cellulitis, warmth, induration |
| Systemic signs | None | Fever, elevated WCC, raised CRP |

### Why chronic inflammation stalls healing

When a wound becomes infected or biofilm persists, the inflammatory phase does not resolve. Neutrophils continue to flood the wound and release **matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs)** in excess. MMPs destroy:

- Fibronectin (the scaffold for cell migration)
- Growth factors (PDGF, TGF-β)
- Newly forming collagen

The result is a wound in a destructive loop: tissue is built and broken down simultaneously, and net healing is zero. This is the core mechanism behind most chronic wounds.

## Practical application

- A wound that looks inflamed on day 1 is behaving correctly — do not suppress inflammation unnecessarily.
- A wound that is still showing cardinal signs at day 10, or that has spreading perilesional erythema, requires infection assessment including wound swab and systemic review.
- Exudate colour and character are key: serous = normal; cloudy or purulent = concern.
- Addressing infection or biofilm is the prerequisite for resolving chronic inflammation — no dressing alone can achieve this while the pathogen load remains high.

## Key takeaways

- Inflammation begins within minutes; its purpose is wound-bed cleaning, not healing per se.
- Histamine and prostaglandins drive vasodilation and vascular permeability, producing exudate.
- Neutrophils dominate days 1–2; macrophages take over from day 2–3 and launch proliferation.
- The four cardinal signs (redness, heat, swelling, pain) are normal and expected.
- Chronic inflammation destroys healing factors via excess MMPs — addressing the cause is essential.
