# Moist Wound Healing

## Learning objectives

- Explain the principle of moist wound healing and its scientific basis
- Describe the benefits of maintaining a moist wound environment
- Identify the risk of excessive moisture and how to manage it
- Apply the principle of moisture balance to dressing selection reasoning

## Core concepts

For most of the twentieth century, clinicians believed wounds healed best when kept dry and exposed to air. In 1962, Dr George Winter's landmark experiment challenged this assumption and established what is now the foundational principle of modern wound care: **wounds heal faster in a moist environment**.

### The science behind moist wound healing

Winter compared wounds in pigs covered with an occlusive film versus wounds left exposed to air. The covered (moist) wounds showed:

- **Re-epithelialisation twice as fast** as uncovered wounds
- **Less pain** — nerve endings remained hydrated rather than desiccating
- **Reduced scarring** — epithelial cells could migrate freely without having to tunnel under a hard eschar

The mechanism is straightforward. Epithelial cells (keratinocytes) migrate across the wound surface. To do this, they need:

1. A moist surface to glide across — dry tissue stops migration
2. Maintenance of the moisture film between the migrating cell sheet and the wound bed
3. Protection from desiccation of the advancing cell edge

In a dry wound, keratinocytes cannot cross the desiccated surface and must instead migrate under the hard eschar where a thin moisture layer is preserved. This is why dry wounds eventually close, but far more slowly.

### Benefits of moist wound healing

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Faster epithelialisation | Keratinocytes migrate freely without burrowing under eschar |
| Autolytic debridement | Moisture activates endogenous proteases and enzymes that liquefy slough and necrotic tissue |
| Pain reduction | Hydrated nerve endings are less sensitised |
| Less scarring | Faster and more ordered closure reduces fibrosis |
| Maintained growth factors | Moist exudate retains growth factors at the wound surface rather than losing them to evaporation |

### Autolytic debridement

One of the most clinically useful consequences of moist wound healing is **autolytic debridement** — the body's own enzymatic removal of devitalised tissue. When a moist environment is created and maintained:

- Endogenous proteases (from macrophages and wound fluid) dissolve necrotic tissue and slough
- This process is selective — healthy tissue is not damaged
- It is painless and does not require sharp instruments

This is why occlusive or semi-occlusive dressings can soften and lift slough from a wound bed over several days without any instrumental debridement.

### The risk of excessive moisture: maceration

While moisture is beneficial, **excess moisture is harmful**. When wound exudate is produced in larger volumes than the dressing can manage:

- **Maceration** occurs — surrounding perilesional skin becomes waterlogged, soft, white, and fragile
- Macerated skin loses its barrier function and is at risk of skin breakdown and wound edge deterioration
- The wound can enlarge rather than contract

Maceration is not caused by moist wound healing — it is caused by **failure to manage moisture balance** appropriately. The correct response is not to dry the wound, but to absorb excess exudate while retaining adequate moisture at the wound surface.

### Moisture balance in practice

Selecting the correct dressing involves matching dressing absorbency to exudate volume:

| Exudate level | Appropriate dressing class | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Low (dry wound) | Hydrating dressings | Hydrogel, hydrocolloid |
| Moderate | Foam, hydrocolloid | Standard foams |
| High | High-absorbency foam, alginates, superabsorbents | Aquacel, DACC, superabsorbent pads |

The MOIST framework (Moisture balance, Oxygen, Infection, Support of healing, Tissue management) formalises this principle as part of wound bed preparation — it will be covered in depth in a later module.

## Practical application

- Never leave a wound exposed to air to "dry out" unless there is a specific clinical reason (e.g. stable heel eschar in an ischaemic limb).
- Assess perilesional skin at every dressing change for signs of maceration — white, soft, fragile skin around the wound edge requires a more absorptive dressing.
- If exudate is high-volume, offensive, or purulent, assess for infection rather than simply increasing absorbency.

## Key takeaways

- George Winter demonstrated in 1962 that moist wounds heal twice as fast as dry wounds.
- Moisture enables free keratinocyte migration and autolytic debridement.
- Excessive moisture causes maceration — the goal is moisture balance, not maximum wetness.
- Dressing selection should match absorbency to exudate volume.
