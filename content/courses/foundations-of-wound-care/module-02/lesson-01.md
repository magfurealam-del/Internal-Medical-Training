# Skin Anatomy and Physiology

## Learning objectives

- Name the three main layers of the skin and describe the structures each one contains.
- Explain the six main functions of the skin.
- Explain why aging skin is more vulnerable to injury and slower to heal.

## Core concepts

The skin is the body's largest organ, covering about 2 square meters, and has three layers:

- **Epidermis** — the outer, thinner layer. It is stratified keratinized squamous epithelium with four cell types: keratinocytes (90% of the epidermis; produce keratin, which waterproofs and protects the skin), melanocytes (produce melanin, giving skin its color and UV protection), Langerhans cells (first-line immune defense), and Merkel cells (touch detection). The epidermis itself has up to five sub-layers: stratum basale (where new keratinocytes form) → stratum spinosum → stratum granulosum → stratum lucidum (thick skin only, e.g. palms/soles) → stratum corneum (outermost, entirely dead cells being shed).
- **Dermis** — the thicker, deeper layer providing structural support, made of collagen (strength) and elastin (elasticity/flexibility). It has two sub-layers: the **papillary layer** (upper, thinner, loose connective tissue, contains dermal papillae that interlock with the epidermis to increase surface area for oxygen/nutrient/waste exchange, houses capillaries, lymph vessels, sensory neurons) and the **reticular layer** (deeper, thicker, dense irregular connective tissue rich in collagen/elastin, houses hair follicles, sweat glands, sebaceous glands, and larger blood vessels).
- **Subcutaneous layer (hypodermis)** — mostly adipose (fat) tissue; cushions internal organs, insulates against temperature change, stores energy, anchors skin to underlying muscle/bone, and carries blood vessels that supply the dermis.

## Practical application

**The six functions of skin** — every one of these is relevant to wound care, because a wound is a breach in all of them at once:

1. **Thermoregulation** — via sweating and blood vessel dilation.
2. **Protection** — a barrier against pathogens and physical injury.
3. **Sensation** — nerve endings detect touch, pain, temperature (loss of this, e.g. in diabetic neuropathy, is why patients don't notice early tissue damage).
4. **Excretion** — removes waste products through sweat.
5. **Vitamin D synthesis** — produced on sun exposure.
6. **Non-verbal communication** — skin color/condition can reflect emotional and physical health.

**Aging skin and wound risk.** As skin ages: collagen and elastin production decreases (thinner skin, more wrinkles, higher skin-tear risk from the same mechanical force that wouldn't tear younger skin), the epidermis regenerates less effectively (slower healing), blood supply is reduced (less resilient tissue), and sebaceous gland activity decreases (drier, more damage-prone skin). This is why the same fall or dressing-adhesive removal that leaves young skin unmarked can cause a serious skin tear in an elderly patient.

**Skin tone and visible signs.** Erythema, cyanosis, and early tissue damage are harder to see in darker skin tones. When visible redness cannot be relied on, use localized warmth, induration/firmness, edema, and patient-reported pain or itching at the site instead — this is essential for accurate early pressure-injury detection in every patient, not just an edge case.

## Key takeaways

- Epidermis (barrier/renewal) → Dermis (strength, elasticity, vascular/nerve supply) → Subcutaneous layer (cushioning, insulation, energy storage) — every wound-care decision traces back to which of these layers is involved (this is what "partial-thickness" vs. "full-thickness" means).
- The skin performs six distinct functions; a chronic wound represents an ongoing failure of several of them simultaneously, not just "a hole in the skin."
- Aging reduces collagen/elastin, epidermal turnover, blood supply, and sebaceous activity together — this combination is why elderly patients skin-tear more easily and heal more slowly.
- Never rely on visible erythema alone in darker skin tones — use warmth, induration, edema, and reported pain/itch instead.
