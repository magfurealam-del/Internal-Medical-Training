# Core Clinical Frameworks: VIP, TIME, MOIST, Wound Balance, and the Triangle of Wound Assessment

## Learning objectives

- Apply the TIME framework to structure wound bed preparation.
- Apply the MOIST framework to structure the wound healing environment.
- Explain how VIP, TIME, MOIST, Wound Balance, and the Triangle of Wound Assessment fit together as one decision sequence rather than five competing systems.

## Core concepts

This course has already introduced **VIP** (Module 5) as the first safety screen — Vascular, Infection/Inflammation, Pressure — applied before any local therapy is chosen. The remaining frameworks build on VIP once it is safe to proceed to local wound bed management.

**TIME** is a systematic approach to wound bed preparation, addressing every critical aspect of local wound healing:

- **T — Tissue management (debridement)**: remove dead, damaged, or infected tissue to allow granulation. Techniques include enzymatic, biological, sharp, and autolytic (moisture-retentive dressing) debridement, chosen based on wound type, depth, and patient condition.
- **I — Inflammation and infection control**: inflammation is a normal part of healing but becomes a barrier when prolonged. Two structured criteria help distinguish severity: **NERDS** (Non-healing wound, Erythema, Radiating pain, Discharge, Systemic signs) for superficial infection, and **STONEES** (Swelling, Tenderness, Oozing, Non-responsive to treatment, Exudate changes, Elevated temperature, Smell) for deep infection. Superficial signs point to topical antimicrobials; deep/STONEES signs point to systemic antibiotics and more aggressive intervention.
- **M — Moisture balance**: match the dressing to the exudate level — hydrogels for dry wounds needing moisture, foam for moderate/heavy exudate, alginates for heavy exudate, hydrocolloids for low/moderate exudate, transparent films to protect while allowing gas exchange. Too wet risks maceration; too dry delays epithelialization.
- **E — Edge of wound advancement**: healthy, advancing edges indicate the wound is progressing toward closure. Non-advancing edges (rolled, undermined, or static) are a signal to reassess tissue, infection, and moisture status rather than simply changing the dressing.

**MOIST** is the complementary framework for the wound's overall healing environment, built on the moist-wound-healing principle introduced in Module 3 (George Winter, 1962): it asks whether **M**oisture is balanced, **O**xygenation/perfusion is adequate, **I**nfection/inflammation is controlled, **S**upport factors (nutrition, offloading, circulation) are addressed, and **T**issue management is current — in effect, a whole-wound-environment checklist that sits alongside TIME's wound-bed-specific steps.

## Practical application

**Wound Balance** extends the same logic to the whole wound, not just the bed: instead of treating tissue, moisture, infection, and edge advancement as separate boxes to tick, it asks whether they are all moving in the same direction at the same time — a wound with clean granulation tissue but excessive exudate and a static edge is not "balanced" even though individual TIME components look fine in isolation. Use Wound Balance as the summary judgment after working through TIME: are all elements consistent with progress, or is one factor undermining the others?

**The Triangle of Wound Assessment** structures the physical exam itself into three zones so nothing is missed: the **wound bed** (tissue type, exudate — Module 4), the **wound edge** (advancing, rolled, undermined — the "E" in TIME), and the **periwound skin** (maceration, dryness, fragility — Module 2's skin-integrity content). Assessing all three zones, not just the bed, is what catches problems like periwound maceration or edge undermining before they become the dominant barrier to healing.

**Putting the five frameworks in sequence**: VIP (is it safe to treat locally at all?) → Triangle of Wound Assessment (examine bed, edge, and periwound systematically) → TIME (structure the wound-bed preparation plan) → MOIST (check the whole healing environment) → Wound Balance (confirm every element is moving toward closure together, not just individually acceptable).

## Key takeaways

- TIME (Tissue, Infection/Inflammation, Moisture, Edge) structures wound-bed preparation; NERDS and STONEES are the criteria for distinguishing superficial from deep infection within the "I."
- MOIST extends the moist-wound-healing principle into a whole-environment checklist: moisture, oxygenation, infection control, support, tissue.
- The Triangle of Wound Assessment (bed, edge, periwound) ensures the physical exam covers all three zones, not just the wound bed.
- Wound Balance is the summary judgment that all elements (tissue, moisture, infection, edge) are progressing together — not five independent checkboxes.
- These five frameworks are one sequential decision process (VIP → Triangle → TIME → MOIST → Wound Balance), not five competing systems to choose between.
