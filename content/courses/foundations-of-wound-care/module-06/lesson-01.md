# Standardized Wound Documentation and Classification Systems

## Learning objectives

- Use standardized wound descriptors so any two clinicians describe the same wound the same way.
- Apply the Wagner Classification and NPUAP pressure injury staging systems correctly.
- State the documentation and photography standards required for auditable, comparable records.

## Core concepts

**Why standardization matters**: without it, two staff members can describe the same wound differently, miss deterioration, choose different products for the same finding, or fail to escalate in time. Standard language is what makes wound outcomes auditable across an entire care team, not just legible to the person who wrote the note.

**Standardized wound descriptors** — every wound record should capture, in this order:

1. **Wound type** — acute vs. chronic (e.g. diabetic foot ulcer, venous ulcer).
2. **Location** — specific body part/anatomical location.
3. **Size and shape** — length, width, depth, and irregularities.
4. **Tissue types present** — necrotic, slough, granulation, epithelialization (Module 4).
5. **Exudate** — type (serous, purulent) and amount (minimal, moderate, heavy).
6. **Infection signs** — erythema, odor, pus, or temperature increase.

**Classification systems** — three systems every clinician should recognize and use correctly:

- **Wagner Classification** (diabetic foot ulcers) — Grades 0–5, based on depth, infection, and gangrene: Grade 0 = pre-ulcerative lesion; Grade 5 = extensive gangrene involving the entire foot.
- **NPUAP Pressure Injury Staging**: Stage 1 = non-blanchable erythema of intact skin; Stage 2 = partial-thickness skin loss (epidermis or dermis); Stage 3 = full-thickness skin loss, may involve subcutaneous fat; Stage 4 = full-thickness tissue loss with exposed bone, tendon, or muscle.
- **University of Texas (UT) System** (diabetic foot ulcers) — combines depth (A–D) and grade (0–3) to additionally account for ischemia and infection, giving a more complete picture than Wagner alone.

## Practical application

**Documentation standard** (per record-keeping guidance): every entry must be legible, dated, and signed, and must protect patient confidentiality. Good documentation is the cornerstone of continuity of care — it is what lets the next clinician on shift trust the wound's trajectory without re-examining from zero.

**Photography standard**: standardize lighting, angle, and equipment/scale reference so photos are comparable over time, and always obtain informed patient consent before taking any photograph. A photo without a consistent reference scale or consistent angle is not useful for tracking change — it's just a picture.

**Putting it together**: a complete, auditable wound record states the standardized descriptors (above), the applicable classification/stage (Wagner grade, NPUAP stage, or UT grade as relevant), and is supported by a consistently framed, consented photograph — this combination is what makes a wound record usable by a different clinician on a different day.

## Key takeaways

- Standardized descriptors (type, location, size, tissue, exudate, infection signs) make wound records comparable between clinicians and over time — this is what documentation is actually for.
- Wagner (DFU), NPUAP (pressure injury), and the UT system (DFU with ischemia/infection) are the three classification systems every clinician should apply correctly and consistently.
- Documentation must be legible, dated, signed, and confidential; photography must be standardized (lighting/angle/scale) and consented.
- A wound record is only auditable if descriptors, classification, and photograph are all present together — any one alone is insufficient.
