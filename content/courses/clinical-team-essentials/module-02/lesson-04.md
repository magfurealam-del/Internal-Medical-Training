# Proliferation: Building New Tissue

## Learning objectives

- Describe the three main processes that occur during the proliferative phase
- Explain the role of fibroblasts, angiogenesis, and wound contraction
- Identify the visual characteristics of healthy versus unhealthy granulation tissue
- Recognise clinical signs that suggest the proliferative phase is failing

## Core concepts

The proliferative phase begins around day 3 after injury and may last up to 14 days in an acute wound. It is the rebuilding phase — the body's mechanism for filling the wound cavity with new vascular tissue and closing the wound surface. Three simultaneous processes drive it: **granulation tissue formation**, **angiogenesis**, and **wound contraction**.

### Granulation tissue formation

Macrophage-released growth factors (PDGF, TGF-β) activate **fibroblasts** — the primary architect cells of the proliferative phase. Fibroblasts migrate into the wound bed and:

1. Lay down a provisional extracellular matrix of collagen (initially type III)
2. Produce fibronectin and hyaluronic acid that support further cell migration
3. Differentiate into **myofibroblasts** that generate the contractile force for wound closure

**Healthy granulation tissue** appears:
- Red to pink
- Moist and glistening
- Granular in texture (the "granules" are new capillary loops)
- Non-friable, not bleeding excessively on light contact

**Unhealthy or stalled granulation** appears:
- Pale or dusky — suggests ischaemia or poor perfusion
- Dark red and extremely friable — suggests infection or high bacterial load
- Flat and "glassy" — suggests hypergranulation or stalled differentiation
- Covered with yellow slough — suggests chronic inflammation has not resolved

### Angiogenesis: building the blood supply

Granulation tissue is useless without oxygen and nutrients. **Angiogenesis** — the formation of new capillary networks — runs in parallel with fibroblast activity. VEGF (vascular endothelial growth factor), released by macrophages and fibroblasts, stimulates endothelial cells to sprout new capillary buds into the wound bed from the wound margins.

These capillary loops are what give granulation tissue its characteristic granular, red appearance under good lighting. Adequate angiogenesis depends on:

- **Adequate oxygen**: hypoxic wounds initially trigger VEGF, but severely ischaemic wounds cannot sustain angiogenesis
- **Adequate nutrition**: protein, zinc, vitamin C, and iron are all needed for vascular repair
- **Freedom from pressure**: capillary loops are fragile and collapse under sustained load

### Wound contraction

As fibroblasts become myofibroblasts, they generate mechanical force that pulls the wound edges inward — **wound contraction**. This is an efficient mechanism: contraction can close up to 30–40% of the wound area without requiring new epithelium.

Contraction should be distinguished from **contracture** — scar contracture is pathological contraction that develops after healing and restricts movement, particularly over joints.

### Epithelialisation: the final step in closure

Once the wound bed is filled with granulation tissue, **keratinocytes** migrate across the surface from the wound margins and any remaining hair follicles. Epithelialisation requires:

- A moist wound surface — keratinocytes cannot migrate across a dry, desiccated bed
- An even, level wound bed — irregular surfaces delay keratinocyte migration
- Freedom from infection — high bacterial load produces toxins that kill migrating keratinocytes

## Practical application

- If the wound bed is pale or avascular, address perfusion and nutrition before changing dressings.
- Yellow slough covering the granulation bed prevents epithelialisation — debridement or autolytic dressings may be required.
- Drying a wound (with air exposure or gauze) stops epithelialisation — keep the bed moist.
- Hypergranulation (raised, "proud flesh" above wound edges) blocks epithelialisation — it requires management before closure can complete.

## Key takeaways

- Proliferation begins around day 3 and lasts up to 14 days in acute wounds.
- Fibroblasts lay collagen and become myofibroblasts that drive wound contraction.
- Angiogenesis brings the blood supply into new granulation tissue.
- Healthy granulation is red, moist, and glistening; pallor or friability signal problems.
- Epithelialisation requires a moist, level, infection-free granulation bed.
